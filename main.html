Simulations of Colloidal Suspensions

Theoretically investigating the soft state of matter

Colloidal suspensions consist of nano- to micro-meter sized particles dispersed in a solvent. These particles experience thermal brownian motion and, being so light, do not sediment, but instead float around - enjoying their entropy - and interact with each other. This results in non trivial collective behaviours which are fascinating and interesting to study not only experimentally but also theoretically, colloids being the perfect playground to use classical statistical mechanics and particle-based computer simulations.

Even more interesting is that new structures and materials can be created by controlling the assembly spontaneously happening in the suspension. One way to do that is to arrange discrete attractive interaction sites ("patches") at prescribed locations on the surface of the colloidal particles, hence the name "patchy particles".

I study the self-assembly processes in different models of patchy particles, identifying which are the important parameters to control the self-assembly and systematically investigating the spontaneously formed structures. To carry on the research, I develop and use different computational techniques such as Monte Carlo (MC) and Molecular Dynamics (MD).

  1. Colloidal Laves phases via complex building blocks

    Is it possible to self-assemble an MgCu₂ Laves phase from complex building blocks? In this paper, we provide evidence that a mixture of hard tetramers and hard spheres could be used to this end. Colloidal tetramers can be synthesised in different ways and used in a sedimentation experiment together with colloidal spheres. Our theoretical results shed light on this self-assembly process, and show unambiguously that it is thermodynamically feasible.

  2. Fluid phase behaviour of "Mickey Mouse" particles

    What are the effects of geometry and interaction range on the fluid phase behaviour of Mickey Mouse particles? In this work, I investigate via computer simulations the interplay between gas-liquid phase separation and self-assembly of MM particles.

  3. "Mickey Mouse" in Vitro and in Silico

    Patchy particles can have funny shapes, like in this case, where they are created in such a way that they resemble the logo of the great Disney's cartoon "Mickey Mouse". Clearly this choice is not driven by nostalgia, but by the idea to stabilise the aggregates formed in solution. Experiments and simulations are combined to show that such particles turn out to be really useful.

  4. Self-Assembly of Patchy Colloidal Dumbbells

    Patchy colloidal dumbbells can be modelled as two spheres fused with each other, where one of the two is attractive. Find out more on the structures these particles can form.
